01

Site & Context

The project is located in Irsee, Bavaria, and is developed for a specific family within a real residential context. The design responds to existing site conditions, local construction practices, and the availability of reclaimed materials.

02

Program & Spatial Organization

The house is designed for a family of four, with a clear and compact layout organised across two levels. The program is divided into living, sleeping, and service areas, allowing for flexible use and future adaptation. The spatial organisation follows the logic of the material system, resulting in a clear and efficient floor plan.

03

Ground Floor

Two distinct volumes separate heated living spaces from unheated service areas. A compact service core at the rear organizes wet functions, while the living spaces open toward the garden. The unheated volume allows for future conversion into a studio apartment.

04

First Floor

The first floor continues the volumetric division. Private rooms are located in the left volume, while the right volume accommodates a separate piano studio.

05

Construction & Section

The construction is based on a modular timber system combined with reclaimed components. Load-bearing walls, floor build-up, and roof structure are assembled using dry connections, enabling simple construction processes and future disassembly. Sections reveal how the system integrates structural logic, material reuse, and spatial organisation.

Developed in collaboration with BAUFRITZ, one of the leading prefabricated timber house companies in Germany, the system is based on the reuse of industrial timber offcuts from their production. Through this partnership, offcuts are collected, cut to module size, and supplied directly from prefabrication processes.

The small scale of the modules allows for an efficient use of otherwise discarded material. By standardising the offcuts into a consistent unit, the system transforms irregular leftovers into a coherent construction logic.

The components are lightweight and designed for dry assembly. A simple template guides the stacking process, enabling construction without specialised labour or heavy machinery. The elements are transported directly to site and assembled through a plug-in system, allowing for a low-tech and accessible building process.

Design Concept

Developed as an independent Bachelor's thesis, the project moves beyond a purely academic design exercise. Instead of responding to a fictional brief, it is conceived as a real-world project for a specific family, integrating design, material sourcing, and organizational processes into a single architectural framework.

The proposal is embedded within the non-profit initiative Supertecture, combining volunteer-based construction with circular building strategies. This context expands the role of the architect from designer to coordinator — involving collaboration with companies, sourcing reclaimed materials, and organizing construction processes beyond the conventional scope of architectural design.

At its core, the project explores a radical approach to material reuse. Based on the principle of "form follows availability," the design is developed entirely from materials already in circulation. Industrial timber offcuts, reclaimed parquet from a church deconstruction, and surplus construction elements define both the spatial organization and the construction logic.

This approach is materialized through the development of a modular building system — the "Home Bricks." These elements transform small-scale timber waste into load-bearing components, enabling a flexible and scalable construction method that can be assembled with limited resources and without specialized labor.

The architecture is deliberately reduced in form and complexity. A clear spatial organization allows the material system to remain legible, while enabling efficient construction and the participation of non-professional builders. The building is slightly elevated, minimizing ground intervention and simplifying assembly.

Rather than optimizing a predefined system, the project tests the boundaries of what is possible within circular construction. It proposes an architecture that is not defined by ideal conditions, but by the realities of available resources, social context, and collective building processes.
